Frequently Asked Questions

Who may benefit from consuming this product?

Adults (Healthy Living 35+) who take care of their bodies and value an active lifestyle. They may struggle to get adequate vitamin D – not only from the diet but also as a result of an indoor lifestyle.

What are potential benefits for using this product as recommended?

If you don’t get enough vitamin D, it can have a big impact on your overall health. Vitamins D and K2 help calcium get into bones, where it belongs. Vitamin D is an essential nutrient that has a role in bone health, heart health and immune system support. Research suggests that vitamin D may help support already normal blood pressure. The active metabolite of vitamin D – calcitriol – supports normal function associated with bone health, cardiovascular health and immune health.†

Take one tablet a day, with or without a meal.

Don’t I get enough vitamin D from sunshine?

Most people don’t, especially in northern latitudes from November through February. The National Institutes of Health recommend 5 to 30 minutes of sun between 10 a.m. and 3 p.m. at least twice a week on the face, arms, legs or back without sunscreen. But that’s just a rough estimate, since it doesn’t take into account cloud cover, shade, pollution and sunscreen use, all of which block the UVB rays from the sun that start the process of vitamin D synthesis in the skin. Because we spend more of our time indoors than previous generations and use more sunscreen when we are out under the sun, almost everybody needs to supplement their diet with vitamin D. The rays of natural sunlight (that generate vitamin D in your skin) cannot penetrate glass. So you don’t generate vitamin D when sitting in your car or home. As people age, they are at increased risk of developing vitamin D insufficiency. This is in part because skin cannot synthesize vitamin D as efficiently, and older adults are likely to spend more time indoors and may have inadequate intakes of the vitamin. Older adults, people with dark pigmented skin and people with limited sun exposure are at increased risk of vitamin D insufficiency.

Don’t I get enough vitamin D from food?

It is challenging to get enough vitamin D from diet alone using unfortified foods, because very few foods in nature contain vitamin D. Cod liver oil does contain high amounts: about 1½ tablespoons per day will provide 2,000 IU. Of commonly consumed foods, the highest in vitamin D is oily fish like salmon, which provides about 100 IU vitamin D per ounce: again, too little to significantly contribute to a daily total of 2,000 IU. Small amounts of vitamin D are found in beef liver, cheese and egg yolks.
